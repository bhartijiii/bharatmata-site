Writing like a human is not about creating a perfect, polished piece, but rather about capturing the imperfections and nuances of human thought. When we write, we don't always think before we write - our thoughts are often in motion, jumping between ideas and reflections.

To mimic this process, we can break up our writing into uneven, unpredictable patterns. This might mean varying the length of our paragraphs, sometimes diving deep into a complex idea and other times summarizing it briefly. It might mean allowing our sentences to wander, meandering through tangents and observations before returning to the main point.

In fact, studies have shown that human writing often feels more authentic when it's a bit messy and imperfect. This is because our brains are wired to think in non-linear, non-sequential ways - we leap from one idea to another, often without fully articulating our thoughts. When we write, we should try to capture this process, rather than striving for perfection and symmetry.

One way to do this is to introduce "narrative drift" - moments where our writing temporarily veers off course, observing something in the environment or recalling a small, unrelated moment. This can help break up the predictability of our writing and make it feel more like a natural, flowing process.

Another way to capture human thought is to use imperfect, mundane examples. Rather than creating polished, concept-driven illustrations, we can use everyday interactions, objects, or moments to illustrate our points. This might mean referencing our own experiences, or those of people around us - experiences that feel real and authentic, rather than artificially constructed.

When we write, we should also allow ourselves to revise and correct our thoughts in real-time. This might mean revising a statement, softening a claim, or rephrasing an idea after expressing it. By doing so, we can create a sense of cognitive imperfection, reflecting the way our brains work in real-time.

Our writing should also reflect our emotional complexity - we shouldn't just present one tone or emotion throughout the piece, but rather shift and evolve as our thoughts and reflections change. This might mean moving from curiosity to skepticism, or from reflection to mild humor. By doing so, we can create a dynamic, engaging reading experience that feels authentic and human.

In terms of structure, we should vary the length and shape of our sections unpredictably. This might mean skipping from one idea to another, or using narrative moments without explanation. By breaking up our writing in this way, we can create a sense of natural flow and variation, rather than relying on predictable, algorithmic patterns.

Finally, our writing should be grounded in the physical world. We should use sensory details to anchor our ideas and create a sense of environmental realism. This might mean referencing visual details, sounds in the background, or physical objects nearby. By doing so, we can create a sense of authenticity and credibility, making our writing feel more like a lived experience rather than an abstract idea.

By following these rules, we can write like humans, capturing the imperfections and nuances of our own thought processes. We can create writing that feels authentic, engaging, and dynamic, reflecting the complexities and messiness of human reflection.
