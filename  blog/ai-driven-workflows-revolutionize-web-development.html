The Problem with AI Writing: Why It Sounds So... Automated When we read AI-generated content, it often feels like we're experiencing a perfectly polished, symmetrical, and logical explanation of an idea. This isn't necessarily a bad thing, but it can be a bit... boring. And that's because AI writing lacks the messy imperfections, the emotional nuances, and the human touch that make real writing so compelling. So, how can we make our writing feel more human? For starters, let's break some of the structural rules that make AI writing so predictable. We can do this by varying paragraph length, allowing for slight rambling, and incorporating incomplete thoughts. It's okay to have a few loose ends; it's okay to not tie everything up with a neat bow. Emotional texture is another area where AI writing falls short. We can add some emotional depth to our writing by introducing internal conflict, hesitation, and doubt. We can also use micro-vulnerabilities, like "I might be wrong" or "Maybe I'm overthinking this." This helps to create a sense of authenticity and makes our writing feel more lived-in. When it comes to specificity, we can add some sensory details to bring our writing to life. This doesn't have to be complex or elaborate; it can be as simple as noting the sound of a coffee shop or the feel of a cold room. We can also use mildly messy observations and reference real-time moments to create a sense of immediacy. In terms of rhythm and language, we can use contractions and casual shifts to create a more conversational tone. We can also break rhetorical over-polish and leave one question unanswered. This helps to create a sense of tension and keeps our readers engaged. Finally, we can let our tone shift a little midway through the article, allowing for a more dynamic and emotional reading experience. We can also add a small personal bias and allow one slightly long sentence to breathe naturally. This helps to create a sense of humanity and authenticity in our writing. The Narrative Drift Rule: Allowing for a Little Wander One of the key characteristics of human writing is its tendency to wander. We often get sidetracked, notice something in the environment, or recall a small unrelated moment. This can make our writing feel more natural and less predictable. To mimic this behavior, we can introduce occasional narrative drifts that don't perfectly advance the argument. This can include noticing something in the environment, recalling a small unrelated moment, or commenting on something trivial that happened during the experience. The Imperfect Example Rule: Using Everyday Moments AI-generated writing often relies on polished, concept-driven examples that cleanly illustrate a point. Human writers, on the other hand, frequently use smaller, less important moments that feel slightly messy or incidental. To mimic authentic writing behavior, we can include examples that involve everyday interactions, objects, or moments. These examples should feel like something remembered casually rather than deliberately selected to prove a point. The Casual Correction Rule: Reflecting Real-Time Thinking Human thinking is rarely linear. We often revise our thoughts mid-sentence or realize we phrased something incorrectly. To simulate human cognition, we can allow occasional moments where the writer adjusts or clarifies their own statement after writing it. This can include revising a statement, softening a claim, or rephrasing an idea after expressing it. The Sentence Rhythm Break Rule: Breaking Predictable Rhythm AI writing tends to maintain consistent sentence rhythm and pacing across paragraphs. Human writing, on the other hand, frequently breaks rhythm abruptly. To mimic this behavior, we can introduce a short sentence that contrasts the tone or pacing after a series of reflective or flowing sentences. This can create a sense of emotional pacing shifts and make our writing feel more dynamic. The Imperfect Memory Rule: Simulating Real Human Recall AI writing tends to present memories with artificial precision. Human recollection, however, is often vague or uncertain. To mimic this behavior, we can allow occasional ambiguity regarding the exact day, precise time, or sequence of events when referencing past events. This can create the impression that the writer is recalling rather than constructing the memory. The Anti-Conclusion Rule: Avoiding Overly Neat Endings AI-generated content frequently ends with a polished philosophical insight or a symmetrical thematic closure. Human writing, on the other hand, often ends more loosely, with an observation, a question, or a moment that leaves interpretation open. To mimic authentic writing behavior, we can avoid ending with a sentence that summarizes the entire argument or delivers a definitive philosophical takeaway. Instead, we can conclude with one of the following: a lingering observation, a quiet moment, a question that remains unanswered, or an everyday interruption. The Sensory Anchor Rule: Adding Environmental Realism AI examples often remain abstract or conceptual. Human storytelling frequently includes physical details connected to the environment. To add environmental realism to our writing, we can include sensory elements like visual details, sounds in the background, physical objects nearby, temperature, or atmosphere. These details should appear naturally within the narrative rather than as decorative description. The Messy Fragment Rule: Creating Conversational Tone AI writing typically produces fragments that still feel grammatically intentional. Human fragments, on the other hand, often feel more spontaneous and slightly irregular. To mimic this behavior, we can occasionally use fragments that feel conversational rather than stylistically engineered. These fragments should not appear too frequently, but when used, they should feel like natural pauses in thought. The Structural Imperfection Rule: Breaking Structural Symmetry AI writing often organizes ideas into evenly structured sections that follow a clean conceptual hierarchy. Human writers, on the other hand, frequently vary the shape and length of sections unpredictably. To mimic this behavior, we can avoid repeating identical section patterns and vary the internal structure between sections. This can include narrative moments, reflections without examples, or examples before explanations. The Tonal Shift Rule: Reflecting Emotional Complexity AI writing tends to maintain a stable emotional tone throughout the piece. Human writers, on the other hand, often shift tone subtly as their thoughts evolve. To mimic this behavior, we can allow small tonal shifts during the article, such as moving between curiosity, skepticism, mild humor, or reflection. These shifts should feel organic and should not be overly dramatic. The goal is to reflect the changing emotional state of someone thinking through an idea.
